The Great Masurbator, Salvador Dali, 1929

Salvador Dali was a Spanish surrealist artist renowned for his technical skill, precise draftsmanship, and the striking and bizarre images in his work.
Flowers, Andy Warhol, 1964

Emerging in the mid 1950s in Britain and late 1950s in America, pop art reached its peak in the 1960s. It began as a revolt against traditional views on what art should be.
Water Lilies, Claude Monet, 1906

During 1870-1890, impressionistic pioneers took the lead to violate the orthodox academism via observation of light and shadow.
Still Life with Fruit Dish, Paul Cezanne, 1879-80

Paul Cezanne is said to have formed the bridge between late 19th-century Impressionism and the early 20th century's new line of artistic enquiry, Cubism.
Yellow-Red-Blue, Wassily Kandisky, 1925

In Germany, from 1910-1914, Wassily Kandisky, as the leader of a German-Expressionist artist group called The Blue Rider, led a new trend of abstract painting around the country through his series of works called Composition.
Fountain, Marcel Duchamp, 1917

Developed in reaction to World War I, the Dada movement consisted of artists who rejected the logic, reason, and aestheticism of modern capitalist society, instead expressing nonsense, irrationality, and anti-bourgeois protest in their works.
Guernica, Pablo Picasso, 1937

During 1907 to 1914, Pablo Picasso founded Cubism, in which artists depicted a subject from a multitude of viewpoints to represent it in a greater context.
Almond Blossom, Vincent van Gogh, 1890

Vincent van Gogh, whose works are characterized by bold colors and dramatic, impulsive brushwork, is a famous leading figure of Expressionism in its early stage.
Dance II, Henri Matisse, 1932

​​In France, from 1905 to 1910, Henri Matisse carried on a Fauvism revolution, known for the intense colors used in paintings.
