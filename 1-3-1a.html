Suprematist Composition, Kazimir Malevich, 1916

In Russia, Suprematism became popular from 1913-1915. It referred to an abstract art based upon the supremacy of pure artistic feeling rather than on visual depiction of objects.
IKB 79, Yves Klein, 1959

Yves Klein began making monochromes in 1947, considering them to be a way of rejecting the idea of representation in painting and therefore of attaining creative freedom conceptually.
Full Fathom Five, Jackson Pollock, 1947

Around the 1950s, Jackson Pollock was a major figure in the abstract expressionist movement and was widely noticed for his technique of splashing liquid household paint onto a surface.
State of Mind III; Those Who Stay, Umberto Boccioni, 1911

In Italy, from 1909-1919, futurism took the stage under rapid industrialization. It emphasized speed, technology, youth, violence, based on a radical state of mind from young Italian artists.
Dancer with Bouquet, Edgar Degas, 1877

Degas is another Impressionist artist especially identified with the subject of bouquet dance; more than half of his works depict dancers.
Death and Life, Gustav Klimt, 1908-15

From 1910-1920, Northern European Expressionist artists have sought to express the meaning of emotional experience rather than physical reality solely from a subjective perspective.
Morning Sun, Edward Hopper, 1952

Edward Hopper was an American realist painter who created subdued drama out of commonplace subjects layered with a poetic meaning, inviting narrative interpretations.
The Two Fridas, Frida Kahlo, 1939

Frida Kahlo is a Mexican painter inspired by the country's popular culture. She employed a naive folk art style to explore questions of identity, postcolonialism, gender, class, and race in Mexican society.
The Son of Man, Rene Magritte, 1964

Rene Magritte was a Belgian surrealist artist, who became well known for creating a number of witty and thought-provoking images.
